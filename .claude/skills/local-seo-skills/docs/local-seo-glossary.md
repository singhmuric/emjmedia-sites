# Local SEO Glossary

Quick reference for every term, acronym, and concept used across the skills in this repository.

---

## Metrics & Measurements

**ARP (Average Rank Position)** — Average ranking across all grid points in a geogrid scan. Scale: 1-20 (1 = #1 everywhere, 20 = not found). Lower is better. Good: under 5. Needs work: 10+.

**ATRP (Average Top Rank Position)** — Average of the top 3 ranking positions in a geogrid scan. Shows best-case performance. When ATRP is much better than ARP, the business ranks well near its location but drops off at distance.

**SoLV (Share of Local Voice)** — Percentage of geogrid points where the business appears in results. 100% = visible everywhere. Strong: 70%+. Moderate: 40-70%. Weak: under 40%. The most client-friendly metric — easy to explain as "what percentage of nearby searchers can find you."

**SAIV (Share of AI Voice)** — Same concept as SoLV but for AI-powered search scans (Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Gemini). Measures how often a business appears in AI-generated answers.

**DA (Domain Authority)** — Moz's 1-100 score predicting how well a domain will rank. Not a Google metric, but useful for competitive benchmarking. Ahrefs uses "DR" (Domain Rating) for a similar concept.

**CPC (Cost Per Click)** — What advertisers pay per click in Google Ads. Even for organic SEO, high CPC indicates high commercial value for a keyword.

**CTR (Click-Through Rate)** — Percentage of people who see a listing and click on it. Map pack position 1 typically gets 25-35% CTR.

**KD (Keyword Difficulty)** — Tool-specific score (Ahrefs, Semrush) estimating how hard it is to rank for a keyword. Scale varies by tool.

**LTV (Lifetime Value)** — Total revenue a customer generates over their relationship with the business. Important for prioritizing keywords — a keyword that brings in high-LTV customers is worth more even with lower volume.

## Google Business Profile (GBP)

**GBP (Google Business Profile)** — Google's free business listing platform. Formerly called Google My Business (GMB). The single most important element in local SEO. Manages the business listing that appears in Google Maps and the local pack.

**GMB (Google My Business)** — Legacy name for Google Business Profile. Rebranded in 2021. Still widely used in the industry.

**GBP API (Google Business Profile API)** — Programmatic interface for managing GBP listings at scale. Requires approval from Google. Used for multi-location management.

**NMX (New Merchant Experience)** — Google's interface for managing a single GBP listing. Accessed at business.google.com.

**Primary Category** — The single most important GBP field for local rankings. Determines which searches the business is most relevant for. Must be the most specific category that matches the business's primary service.

**Additional Categories** — Secondary categories in GBP that expand what the business is relevant for. Typically 3-5 additional categories beyond the primary.

**Attributes** — Structured data fields in GBP like "Women-owned," "Wheelchair accessible," "Free Wi-Fi." Varies by category. Affects both ranking and conversion.

**GBP Posts** — Short content updates published to a GBP listing. Types: Update, Event, Offer. Posts expire after 6 months (events expire after the event date). Weekly posting is a ranking signal.

**Q&A** — Question and answer section on a GBP listing. Businesses can seed their own questions and provide answers. Visible to all searchers.

**Business Description** — 750-character text field in GBP. Minimal direct ranking impact but affects conversion. Should include primary services and location naturally.

**GBP Suspension** — Google disabling a business listing for guideline violations. Soft suspension: listing exists but is unverified. Hard suspension: listing is removed. Recovery requires filing a reinstatement request with evidence.

**Suggested Edits** — Changes proposed by Google, users, or third parties to a GBP listing. Can alter business name, hours, phone, or even mark a business as closed. Must be monitored and rejected if incorrect.

**Verification** — The process of proving to Google that you're the legitimate owner of a business. Methods: postcard, phone, email, video, or instant verification. Unverified listings rank poorly.

## Search Results & SERPs

**SERP (Search Engine Results Page)** — The page Google displays after a search. Contains organic results, ads, local pack, featured snippets, People Also Ask, and other features.

**Map Pack / Local Pack / 3-Pack** — The map + 3 business results that appear in local search results. The primary target of local SEO. Appears above organic results for queries with local intent.

**Local Finder** — The expanded local results page when a user clicks "More places" in the map pack. Shows 20+ results with a full map. Rankings here can differ from the 3-pack.

**Organic Results** — The standard "10 blue links" (non-ad, non-map) search results. Local businesses can rank in both the map pack AND organic results simultaneously.

**Featured Snippet** — A highlighted answer box at the top of organic results. Occasionally relevant for local businesses with informational content.

**People Also Ask (PAA)** — A box of related questions in search results. Each question expands to show an answer sourced from a web page. Good for identifying content opportunities.

**AI Overview (AIO)** — Google's AI-generated summary that appears at the top of some search results. Increasingly common for local queries. Businesses appearing in AIOs get visibility even without traditional ranking.

**Local Intent** — A search query that Google interprets as wanting local results. Can be implicit ("plumber" — no location but still local) or explicit ("plumber buffalo ny").

**Near Me Query** — A search including "near me" which Google resolves based on the searcher's GPS or IP location. Example: "pizza near me." These have grown exponentially and have no specific location to target — businesses rank based on proximity to the individual searcher.

**Zero-Click Search** — A search where the user gets their answer directly on the SERP without clicking any result. GBP listings that show phone number, hours, and address enable zero-click actions (calls, directions).

## Business Types

**SAB (Service Area Business)** — A business that travels to customers rather than serving them at a physical location. Hides its address in GBP. Examples: plumbers, electricians, house cleaners. Ranking is based on service area centroid, not a pin.

**Storefront** — A business with a physical location that customers visit. Displays its address in GBP. Examples: restaurants, retail stores, medical offices.

**Hybrid** — A business that both serves customers at its location and travels to them. Shows address AND defines a service area in GBP. Examples: HVAC companies with a showroom, landscapers with a nursery.

**Practitioner** — An individual professional (doctor, lawyer, dentist) who may have their own GBP listing separate from the practice they work at. Google allows this when the practitioner operates independently at the location.

**Multi-Location** — A business with more than one physical location. Each location gets its own GBP listing and must be optimized independently.

**Franchise** — A multi-location business where individual locations are independently owned. Introduces unique challenges: corporate brand guidelines vs. local optimization, shared vs. independent websites.

## Citations & NAP

**NAP (Name, Address, Phone)** — The three core data points that identify a business. Must be character-for-character consistent across all online presences. NAP inconsistency is one of the most common local SEO problems.

**Citation** — Any mention of a business's NAP on a third-party website. Can be structured (directory listing) or unstructured (mention in a blog post or news article).

**Structured Citation** — A business listing on a directory site with organized fields for name, address, phone, website, hours, etc. Examples: Yelp, BBB, Yellow Pages.

**Unstructured Citation** — A mention of business NAP in running text on a non-directory site. Examples: news articles, blog mentions, event listings.

**Data Aggregator** — A company that collects business data and distributes it to hundreds of downstream directories. The major aggregators are Data Axle, Neustar/Localeze, and Foursquare. Submitting to aggregators is more efficient than updating individual directories.

**Data Axle** — Major data aggregator (formerly Infogroup). Feeds business data to directories, GPS systems, and other platforms.

**Neustar/Localeze** — Major data aggregator. Now part of TransUnion.

**Foursquare** — Data aggregator and check-in platform. Powers location data for Apple Maps, Uber, and many other platforms.

## Technical SEO

**Schema Markup / Structured Data** — Code added to a website that tells search engines exactly what the content means. For local SEO, `LocalBusiness` schema (and its subtypes) confirms business details in machine-readable format. Implemented as JSON-LD in a `<script>` tag.

**JSON-LD** — JavaScript Object Notation for Linked Data. The format Google prefers for schema markup. Placed in a `<script type="application/ld+json">` tag, usually in the page `<head>`.

**LocalBusiness Schema** — The schema.org type for local businesses. Has specific subtypes: `MedicalOrganization`, `LegalService`, `Restaurant`, `HomeAndConstructionBusiness`, etc. Always use the most specific subtype.

**E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)** — Google's quality framework for evaluating content. Particularly important for YMYL (Your Money Your Life) topics like medical and legal content.

**Core Web Vitals (CWV)** — Google's page experience metrics: LCP (Largest Contentful Paint), INP (Interaction to Next Paint), CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift). Affects both organic and local rankings.

**Canonical URL** — The "preferred" version of a page when multiple URLs have similar or identical content. Prevents duplicate content issues.

**Hreflang** — HTML attribute that tells Google which language/region a page targets. Relevant for businesses serving multilingual markets.

**Robots.txt** — A file that tells search engines which parts of a site to crawl or not crawl. Misconfigured robots.txt can block important pages from being indexed.

**XML Sitemap** — A file listing all important URLs on a site, helping search engines discover and crawl pages. Should include all location and service pages.

## Advertising

**LSA (Local Services Ads)** — Google's pay-per-lead ad format for local service businesses. Appears at the very top of search results with a "Google Guaranteed" or "Google Screened" badge. Businesses pay per lead (call or message), not per click.

**Google Guaranteed** — A badge on LSAs indicating the business has passed Google's background check and licensing verification. For home service businesses.

**Google Screened** — Similar to Google Guaranteed but for professional services (lawyers, financial advisors, real estate agents). Requires license verification.

**PPC (Pay Per Click)** — Traditional Google Ads model where advertisers pay each time someone clicks their ad. Different from LSAs (pay per lead).

**Google Ads (formerly AdWords)** — Google's paid advertising platform. Local businesses typically run search ads targeting geo-modified keywords and/or display ads targeting local audiences.

**Local Campaign** — A Google Ads campaign type that promotes a physical business location across Search, Maps, Display, and YouTube.

## Geogrid & Rank Tracking

**Geogrid** — A grid of GPS coordinates overlaid on a map, used to check rankings from multiple locations simultaneously. Reveals how rankings vary across geography. The foundation of local rank tracking.

**Grid Point** — A single coordinate in a geogrid where a ranking check is performed. A 7×7 grid has 49 grid points.

**Grid Size** — The dimensions of a geogrid. Common sizes: 3×3 (9 points), 5×5 (25), 7×7 (49), 9×9 (81), 11×11 (121), 13×13 (169), 15×15 (225).

**Radius** — The distance from the center of a geogrid to its outer edge. Determines how large a geographic area the scan covers.

**Centroid** — The center point of a geogrid, typically placed at the business's physical location. For SABs, Google calculates an internal centroid based on service area settings.

**Heatmap** — A visual representation of geogrid rankings using color coding. Green = ranking well (positions 1-3), Yellow = moderate (4-7), Orange = weak (8-13), Red = poor (14-20), Gray = not found.

**Rank Tracking** — Monitoring where a business ranks for specific keywords over time. Local rank tracking requires geogrid methodology because rankings vary by location.

**Competitor Report** — A report showing which businesses rank at each grid point, revealing who dominates which geographic areas.

**Trend Report** — Historical ranking data for the same keyword/location/grid combination over multiple scans. Shows improvement or decline over time.

## Reviews

**Review Velocity** — The rate at which a business receives new reviews (typically measured monthly). Consistent velocity is better than bursts.

**Review Gating** — The (prohibited) practice of screening customers before asking for reviews — only sending happy customers to Google. Violates Google guidelines.

**Review Schema** — Structured data markup for reviews/ratings. Google has strict rules about which review types can use schema — self-serving reviews (reviews of your own business) cannot use review schema on your own website.

**Sentiment Analysis** — Analyzing review text to determine whether the overall tone is positive, negative, or neutral. Useful for identifying trends and common complaints.

**Owner Response** — The business's reply to a review on GBP. Visible to all future searchers. A confirmed ranking factor when done consistently.

## Links & Authority

**Backlink** — A link from another website to your website. A core ranking factor for both organic and local search.

**Local Link** — A backlink from a locally relevant source: local news sites, community organizations, chamber of commerce, local business partners. More valuable for local SEO than generic links.

**Link Building** — The process of acquiring backlinks to improve search rankings. For local businesses, focus is on local relevance over raw authority.

**Anchor Text** — The clickable text of a link. For local SEO, natural anchor text mentioning the business name or services is ideal.

**Referring Domain** — A unique website that links to your site. 50 links from 50 different domains is stronger than 50 links from 1 domain.

## Platforms & Tools

**Local Falcon** — Geogrid rank tracking platform. Scans rankings across a geographic grid and provides ARP, ATRP, and SoLV metrics. Supports Google Maps, Apple Maps, and AI search platforms.

**LSA Spy** — Tool for monitoring Local Services Ads rankings across markets. Tracks which businesses appear in LSA results, their positions, and changes over time.

**SERP API / SerpAPI** — APIs that return search engine results programmatically. Used to check rankings, analyze SERPs, and monitor search features.

**BrightLocal** — Local SEO platform offering citation building, rank tracking, review management, and audit tools.

**Whitespark** — Local SEO tools specializing in citation finding, building, and local rank tracking.

**Semrush** — All-in-one SEO platform with keyword research, rank tracking, site audit, and competitive analysis. Has local-specific features.

**Ahrefs** — SEO tool focused on backlink analysis and keyword research. DR (Domain Rating) is its authority metric.

**DataForSEO** — API providing keyword data, SERP results, and various SEO metrics. Used for building custom tools and reports.

**Screaming Frog** — Desktop crawler for technical SEO audits. Crawls websites and reports on technical issues affecting search performance.

**Google Search Console (GSC)** — Free Google tool showing how a site performs in Google Search. Provides click data, impressions, indexing status, and technical issues.

**Google Analytics (GA4)** — Free Google analytics platform tracking website traffic, user behavior, and conversions.

## Industry Terms

**HIPAA** — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act. US law requiring protection of patient health information. Critical for healthcare businesses responding to reviews — cannot confirm or deny someone is a patient or reference any care details.

**YMYL (Your Money Your Life)** — Google's classification for content that could impact a person's health, finances, or safety. Medical, legal, and financial content is held to higher quality standards.

**Vanity Number** — A phone number using letters (e.g., 518-324-PAIN). Creates citation issues because directories may store the numeric equivalent differently than the display format.

**Tracking Number** — A phone number used to track call sources (common in marketing). Can create NAP inconsistency if the tracking number differs from the main business number across directories.

**Keyword Stuffing** — Artificially inserting keywords into content, business names, or review responses. In business names, it violates GBP guidelines but is common because it works — until Google penalizes it.

**Pigeon Update** — Google's 2014 algorithm update that heavily changed local search rankings. Tightened the connection between local and organic signals.

**Possum Update** — Google's 2016 local algorithm update that improved result diversity based on the searcher's location and helped businesses just outside city limits rank for city searches.

**Vicinity Update** — Google's December 2021 local algorithm update that significantly increased the weight of proximity as a ranking factor. Reduced the ranking radius for many businesses.
